<TextBlock label="Context" heading="Finding the right thing is a design problem, not just an algorithm problem.">
  The product had strong supply and strong demand. The gap was signal. Users landed on a homepage with 60+ options, no clear entry point, and recommendations that felt generic enough to be useless.

  The brief was to improve conversion. The real problem was trust. We needed to make users feel the product understood them before they'd committed to anything.
</TextBlock>

<MediaBlock caption="Existing state audit — homepage discovery flow, showing decision fatigue and undifferentiated listings." />

<TextBlock label="Research" heading="Watching people get stuck.">
  We ran five days of moderated sessions across three markets. The pattern was consistent: users would scroll, hesitate, open a few options, and close the app. Not because the supply wasn't there. Because nothing felt like it was *for them*.

  Two signals stood out from session analysis:

  - Users who filtered by price converted at 2× the rate of those who didn't
  - Users who saw a result matching a previous search pattern converted at 3× rate

  The personalisation data existed. It wasn't being used at the surface.
</TextBlock>

<CalloutBlock metric="3×" label="Conversion lift from contextual relevance vs. unpersonalised results">
  Users weren't looking for more options. They were looking for *the right* option — and they could tell, within seconds, when the surface wasn't going to give it to them.
</CalloutBlock>

<TextBlock label="Design" heading="From catalogue to conversation.">
  We moved away from a static grid to a ranked, scrollable feed with contextual clusters. Instead of filtering, users could refine through interaction — the more they engaged, the more the surface adapted.

  The core design decisions:

  - Replaced the filter drawer with an inline refinement bar that surfaced relevant parameters dynamically
  - Introduced a "for you" cluster at the top of results, with a lightweight explanation of why items appeared
  - Reduced the number of options shown on load from 24 to 8 — forcing curation, improving perceived quality
</TextBlock>

<MediaPairBlock caption="Left: legacy browse grid. Right: personalised feed with contextual clusters." />

<TextBlock label="AI Integration" heading="Making the model legible.">
  The ML recommendations were already running. The problem was that they were invisible — indistinguishable from editorial or paid placements.

  We designed a lightweight disclosure layer: short, plain-language explanations ("Because you searched last week", "Popular in this area for your dates") that surfaced the reasoning without exposing the model.

  This single change — transparency over black-box output — increased engagement with recommended items by 28%.
</TextBlock>

  - **+28% engagement** with AI-recommended items after disclosure layer
  - Became the primary acquisition surface within two quarters
  - Foundational pattern adopted by two adjacent product teams

  My contribution: end-to-end design ownership from research through to QA. Managed a team of two mid-level designers. Worked directly with ML, data, and three engineering squads across two time zones.
